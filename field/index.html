CHAPTER 1

ECONOMICS

Economics is defined as teh study of scarcity and its implications for the use of resources, production of goods and services, growth of production and welfare over time.

Branches of Economics

There are two (2) main branches of Economics:
1. Macro Economics
2. Micro Economics

Macro Economics

Macro Economics also referred to as economics of nations is a branch of economics which deals with performance, structure, behavior and decision making of an economy as a whole.

Issues of macro economics

1. Inflation: This is a measure of the rate of rising prices of goods and services in an economy. The types of inflation are demand-pull inflation and cost-pull inflation.
2. Unemployment: This is a term referring to individuals who are employable and actively seeking a job but are unable to find a job. The types of unemployment are cyclical unemployment, structural unemployment and frictional unemployment.

Micro Economics

This is a branch of economics that studies individuals, households and firms' behaviour in decisioni making and allocation of resources.

CHAPTER 2

ECONOMIC GROUPS

These are organisations that encourage economic cooperation among its members.

They consist mainly of:
1. consumers 2. producers Consumers purchase goods and services and also pay taxes. Producers receive legal tenders in exchange of goods and services. Examples of legal tenders are the British pounds, Indian rupees, Bangladesh taka, etc.

FACTORS OF PRODUCTION

There are four (4) main factors of production namely:


1. Land: This refers to the natural resources used to create goods and services.
2. Labour: This refers to the effort that contributes to the production of goods and services.
3. Capital: This refers to the machinery, tools and buildings used to produce goods and services.
4.Entrepreneur: An entrepreneur is a person who combines land, labour and capital to produces goods and services with the aim of making profits.

CHAPTER 3

ECONOMICS APPROACHES

Over the years, different people, societies and institutions have come up with several ways of understanding and practicing economics. Certain individuals made names for themselves in their understanding of economics and championed varying schools of thoughts.


1. Behavoural economics: Behavioural economics studies the effects of psychological, cognitive, emotional, cultural and social factors in the decisions of individuals and institutions. Richard Thaler was a well known behavioural economists.
2. Classical economics: Classical economics asserts that economies are self correcting and function best with minimal government intervention. Some prominent classical economists are Adam Smith, David Ricardo, William BLake.
3. Experimental economics: Experimental economics employ the use of controlled scientific experiments to test what choices people actually make in specific circumstances. Abhijit Banerjeee is a well known experimental economist.
4. Financial economics: Financial economists analyse the use and distribution of resources in markets. It involves the creation of sophisticated models to test the variables affecting a particular decision. Examples of financial economists Gita Gapinato, Raghuran Raja, Richard Thaler
5. Keynesians: Keynesians justify government intervention through public policies that aim to achieve price stability and full employment.

CHAPTER 4

MARKETS

A market is simply anywhere goods and services can be bought or exchanged. There are several types of markets:

1. Monopolistic competition

This exists when companies offer competing products or services that are similar but not perfect substitutes. Eg. Burger King and McDonald's


2. Monopoly

A market structure where a single seller sells a unique product in the market. The seller faces no competition. Eg. Microsoft and windows, pharmaceuticals


3. Monopsony

In a monopsony, there is only one buyer. Eg. a coal factory in a coal mining town.

4. Oligopoly

This is a market structure controlled by small number of firms none of which can keep the other from significant market powerEg. auto industry, commercial air travel.

5. Perfect market

This is a theoretical market in which sellers are so numerous and well informed that monopoly is absent and market prices cannot be manipulated.

END OF CHAPTER 4

CHAPTER 5

NATIONAL ORGANISATIONS

These are organisations which work to protect the interest of the nation. An example is the central bank.

Central banks

A central bank is a public institution that manages the currency of a country or group of countries and controls the supply of money.


Functions of the central bank

1. To stabilise a nation's currency.


2. To keep unemployment low.


3. To prevent inflation.


International organisations

The work of international economic organisations goes beyond national borders. Examples are IMF, World bank, WTO.

International Monetary Fund (IMF)

This is an organisation of 190 countries working to foster global monetary cooperation, secure financial stability, facilitate international trade, promote high employment and sustainable economic growth and reduce poverty around the world.


World Bank

It is made up of 189 countries and provides loans and grants to th government of low and middle income countries for the purpose of pursuing capital projects.


World Trade Organisation

It is an international organisation that oversees the rules for global trade among nations and mediates disputes among its member nations.

CHAPTER 6

POLICIES OF ECONOMICS

There are three (3) main policies of economics:

1. Fiscal policies

This is the use of government spending and taxation to influence economic conditions, especially macroeconomic conditions to promote strong and sustainable growth and reduce poverty.


2. Monetary policies

This is an economic policy that manages the size and growth rate of the money supply in an economy.


3. Trade policies

Trade policies determing the mobility of goods and services along with factors of production across national borders through currency restriction and exchange rates.
